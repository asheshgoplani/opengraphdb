# Artificial Intelligence Overview

## Introduction to AI

Artificial intelligence (AI) is the simulation of human intelligence processes by computer
systems. The field was founded as an academic discipline in 1956 and has experienced several
waves of optimism, followed by periods of reduced funding and interest (known as AI winters),
and then new enthusiasm and investment. AI research has tried and discarded many different
approaches, including simulating the brain, modeling human problem solving, formal logic,
large databases of knowledge, and imitating animal behavior.

## Types of Artificial Intelligence

### Narrow AI

Narrow AI, also called weak AI, is AI that is designed and trained for a specific task. Virtual
assistants such as Apple's Siri are a form of narrow AI. Narrow AI systems perform well within
their predefined domains but cannot transfer their skills to other domains. Most current AI
systems, including image recognition, natural language processing, and recommendation engines,
are forms of narrow AI.

### General AI

General AI, or artificial general intelligence (AGI), refers to a machine with general cognitive
abilities. When presented with an unfamiliar task, a strong AI system would be able to use fuzzy
logic to apply knowledge from one domain to another and find a solution autonomously. AGI remains
a theoretical goal and has not yet been achieved. Researchers debate whether AGI is achievable and
what form it might take.

### Superintelligent AI

Superintelligent AI would surpass human intelligence across virtually every field, including
scientific creativity, general wisdom, and social skills. Superintelligence is a topic of
philosophical and ethical debate, with thinkers such as Nick Bostrom and Eliezer Yudkowsky
arguing about its risks and implications for humanity.

## Applications of AI

### Natural Language Processing

Natural language processing (NLP) involves training computers to interpret and generate human
language. Applications include machine translation, sentiment analysis, chatbots, and large
language models. Modern NLP systems rely heavily on transformer architectures and deep learning
techniques, which are branches of machine learning.

### Computer Vision

Computer vision enables machines to interpret visual information from cameras and images. It
powers facial recognition, autonomous vehicles, medical image analysis, and industrial quality
inspection. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have been the dominant architecture for computer
vision tasks since the AlexNet breakthrough in 2012.

### Robotics

Robotics combines AI with physical systems to create machines that can interact with the physical
world. Robots use AI for navigation, object manipulation, and decision-making. Industrial robotics
has been used for decades, while newer applications include surgical robots, delivery drones, and
autonomous vehicles.

## AI and Machine Learning

Machine learning is a subset of AI that focuses on building systems that learn from data. Rather
than being explicitly programmed, machine learning systems improve through experience. The
relationship between AI and machine learning is foundational: most modern AI applications are
built on machine learning techniques including supervised learning, unsupervised learning, and
reinforcement learning.

## AI and Graph-Based Approaches

Graph-based AI methods have gained prominence due to their ability to model relational data.
Knowledge graphs encode real-world entities and their relationships, enabling reasoning about
connections between concepts. Graph neural networks (GNNs) extend deep learning to graph
structured data, enabling AI systems to leverage relational structure. Graph databases provide
efficient storage and retrieval for these graph-structured AI applications.

## Ethics and Safety

As AI systems become more capable and pervasive, ethical concerns have grown. Issues include
algorithmic bias, where AI systems reflect and amplify biases in training data; privacy concerns,
where AI systems collect and analyze large amounts of personal data; and transparency, where
complex AI models are difficult to interpret or explain. Organizations such as the AI Safety
Institute and research groups at major universities study how to build AI systems that are
aligned with human values and resistant to unintended behaviors.
